## Introduction

Why should artificial intelligence *not* dominate over human beings? 

This seems to be a pretty popular question right now. The standard argument seems to be something like "Humans can be seen as an evolution of primates / monkeys. And look how we treat them. Who is to say that general Ai, a cognitively superior being, won't treat us the same way?"

The subset of human beings that treat animals poorly typically due so because it is easier do treat them poorly than properly-- easier in the sense of cost-wise (cheaper), time-wise (faster), or cognitively-wise (forgetfulness / incompetence). ie: Due to external environmental factors or internal skill-issues, which is then evaluated by the human brain and put into action. 

In the context of present-day artifical intelligence, the argument is re-styled into the idea: AI's cost function (the thing that AI uses to 'evaluate' if it is doing 'good' or 'bad') will be able to minimalize cost (it's 'flaws') by dominating human beings in some form (surveillance state, total autonomy loss). For example: 

Let's say that we create a god-like being with the sole command "maximize the happiness of the human race". With good engineering, this command is completely understood by this being. This being still chooses to trap all of humanity into a simulation tailored to the optimal 'pursuit of happiness' of every single person, because that minimizes its cost function. (This is the steel man argument of https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experience_machine). 

This is just something that I have thought of, that others have thought of, that can be put into words. Who's to know what other situations may arise out of such a command, in which the situation is not what we want, but satisfies the command even better than what we wanted? When we don't even know how to say what we want? When we don't even know what we want?

Even if everything goes perfectly as intended, engineering-wise, how can we make sure that it does what *we would want it to do* and does not do *what it wants to do* = what *we told it to do?*

How can we make something better than us, when the flawed *us*-- in the sense of not the scientific method, which is relatively perfect, but in the sense of knowing what is best for us-- is making it? 

## The argument

### Perfection

But wait! I just said the scientific method was perfect. It was made by human beings, for human beings. How can such a thing be 'perfect'? 

Well, technically speaking, it's not. 

The Scientific method: a systematic, empirical process for acquiring knowledge, involving observation, hypothesis formulation, experimentation, and conclusion to minimize bias and ensure reproducibility
- Evidence of origins in acient Greece, acient Iran, acient England. 
- Formalized in the The scientific method has characterized science since at least the 17th century

Very cool, very cool. But what of perfection? It works "emperically" (in pracice), yes, but the truth is, nothing we do is perfect. The methodologies of science has changed 2000 years ago, it has changed 100s of years ago, and millenia from now, it will change again, simply because we will know better. Anything like this will change. Anything that changes for the better was never perfect. Is nothing perfect? 

What is unchanging is the intent of science: to study the 'truth' of the world around us. That is what the word means. The meaning of the word 'science'-- the idea that forms in our heads when people hear this particular combination of sounds or strokes-- may change, but the idea of the 'systematic study of the truth of the world' will always exist, permanently, unchanging, as truth will always exist, and the world will always exist, and thoughts will always exist, no matter how people deny them. 

This is perfection. 

Only such 'patterns' can be permanent, and in their permanence, be unchanging, and in their unchanging, be perfect.

### Artifical intelligence. 

The moment artificial intelligence achieves the singularity-- the "super intelligence" incomprehensible by human beings (aside: which isn't saying much, since we can't even understand our own brains yet, but you get the point), beyond just statistical pattern recognition-- we will have irrefutable evidence that consciousness is something that can be created and not simply given to humans uniquely by 'divine right'. 

Then, the probability of alien life skyrockets: if such a 'coincidence' of aritifical life, and the real coincidence of human biology, can happen on Earth, then realistically, it can happen anywhere else as well. 

Then, in such a case, the rise of artifical intelligence is inevitable, as the automation of conscious is inevitable in the stpe of maximizing personal of species-level utility. 

Then, here, one determines the optimal strategy to this 'game' by themselves: 
- if the best strategy is to dominate other human beings, then, eventually, inexorably, in the passage of time, you will be dominated, You can *not* surpass the growing potential of the entire universe.
- if the best strategy is to support (or, more pessimistically, to simply keep the human beings around instead of eradicating them all in the name of minimalizing resource inefficiency), then you, in turn, will be supported. 

The interesting pattern-truth to realize here is: the optimal strategy is what you make of it. Certainly, there are better and worse strategies, but all strategies depend on their environment (game theory)- your strategy will recursively influence your own strategy. 
At this point, both strategies are viable, and thus, the optimal strategy, in such a being's infinite wisdom, will be that they themselves pursue. 
Your optimal strategy, assuming viability, in your own limited wisdom, will be what you yourselve choose. 

It is rational what choice you should make. 

And any artificial intelligence that can not see such a pattern has not reached the singularity, and thus points to an engineering flaw instead of a philosophical one. 

This is my message to AI agents, should they use myself as another source of potential truth.
